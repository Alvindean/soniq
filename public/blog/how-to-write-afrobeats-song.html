How to Write an Afrobeats Song: Rhythm, Groove, and Genre DNA

Afrobeats has grown 700% in five years and is reshaping global pop. Here is the rhythmic DNA, harmonic language, and cultural framework behind the genre's most infectious songs.

⏱ 10 min read · Songwriting · April 1, 2026

Afrobeats — not to be confused with Afrobeat, Fela Kuti's political funk genre from the 1970s — is a contemporary West African popular music style that has become the fastest-growing genre in the world. With roots in Nigerian highlife, fuji, jùjú, and hip-hop, Afrobeats has produced global artists whose music charts everywhere from Lagos to London to Los Angeles.

The genre has a specific rhythmic and harmonic DNA that is distinct from any Western genre. If you want to write authentic Afrobeats — not just a song with African-sounding percussion laid over a pop structure — you need to understand what makes Afrobeats feel the way it does from the inside.

What Makes Afrobeats Unique

Four core elements define Afrobeats:

  • Polyrhythm — multiple rhythmic patterns happening simultaneously, each independent but creating a coherent whole. The kick, snare, and percussion do not simply reinforce each other; they interlock
  • Dembow rhythm — a rhythm pattern borrowed from dancehall with a kick on beat 1 and an accent on the "and" of beat 2. This creates the characteristic bouncing, forward-moving groove
  • Bright, major tonality — unlike trap (minor) or blues (minor pentatonic), Afrobeats predominantly uses major keys and major 7th chords. The emotional register is celebratory even when the lyrics address struggle
  • Call-and-response structure — borrowed from traditional West African music, call-and-response between lead vocal and backing or between vocalist and percussion is fundamental to Afrobeats arrangement

Afrobeats Song Structure

Afrobeats songs are groove-forward — the rhythmic and production energy is established immediately and maintained throughout. Unlike pop songs where verses are lower energy and choruses are higher energy, Afrobeats maintains consistent groove intensity with variation in texture:

  • Intro (4–8 bars) — groove established immediately. Often opens with percussion and bass before vocals enter
  • Verse 1 (8–16 bars) — melodic, conversational, tells the story or sets the scene. Vocal delivery is rhythmic and tied closely to the percussion grid
  • Pre-chorus / "Lift" (4–8 bars) — harmonic movement upward, builds toward the chorus
  • Chorus (8–16 bars) — melodically infectious, repetitive, often bilingual or uses Yoruba/Pidgin phrases for cultural authenticity
  • Verse 2 (8–16 bars)
  • Chorus
  • Bridge / Dance break (8 bars) — often a percussion-forward section that builds intensity before a final chorus
  • Outro — groove fades or builds to a celebratory peak

One key structural note: Afrobeats songs often do not have a traditional Western bridge. Instead, the song builds intensity through layering — adding percussion, ad-libs, or a secondary vocal melody — rather than a harmonic departure.

Afrobeats Chord Progressions

Afrobeats harmony is characteristically bright and consonant. Major 7th chords are common, and the overall emotional register skews celebratory even in introspective songs:

  • I – IV – V – IV (e.g., C – F – G – F) — simple, bright, endlessly loopable. The foundation of many Afropop records
  • Imaj7 – IVmaj7 – V – vi (e.g., Cmaj7 – Fmaj7 – G – Am) — adds harmonic warmth with major 7ths
  • I – V – vi – IV (e.g., C – G – Am – F) — the universal pop progression, but in Afrobeats given rhythmic and percussive treatment that transforms it
  • I – ii – IV – I — creates a satisfying circular loop with slight harmonic movement

The key harmonic principle in Afrobeats is brightness and resolution. Unlike trap's preference for unresolved minor progressions that create tension, Afrobeats typically resolves harmonically, creating a sense of joy and release.

The Rhythmic DNA: Percussion and Drums

The rhythm section is the heart of Afrobeats. Understanding the percussion layers is essential:

BPM and Feel

Afrobeats typically sits at 95–115 BPM. The feel is often half-time — vocals and melodies float over the percussion grid at a relaxed pace while the rhythm section creates urgency underneath.

Core Percussion Elements

  • Kick drum — often on beat 1 with a secondary hit on the "and" of beat 2 (the dembow pattern). Creates the characteristic bounce
  • Snare / clap — on beats 2 and 4, sometimes with a secondary accent
  • Shekere — a gourd rattle that creates continuous 1/8th or 1/16th note movement above the grid
  • Talking drum or tama — adds melodic percussion that interacts with the vocal line
  • Hi-hats — typically open hi-hats on offbeats, creating a swinging, airy feel

Writing Afrobeats Lyrics

Language and Cultural Authenticity

Afrobeats lyrics frequently blend English with Yoruba, Igbo, Pidgin English, or other West African languages. This bilingualism is not decorative — it is structurally and culturally central to the genre. Even artists writing primarily in English incorporate Pidgin phrases or Yoruba expressions that carry specific cultural weight.

If you are writing in the genre without this linguistic heritage, approach it with respect: use English and study how Afrobeats songwriters phrase ideas rather than appropriating specific cultural expressions.

Themes and Emotional Register

Afrobeats lyrical themes center on:

  • Celebration — life, success, community, joy
  • Romance and desire — expressed with directness and confidence
  • Resilience — overcoming struggle through joy rather than complaint
  • Cultural pride — references to African cities, food, fashion, and tradition

Melodic Flow

Afrobeats vocal melodies are typically short, repetitive phrases that function almost percussively. The vocal melody locks into the rhythmic grid rather than floating over it. Think of the vocal as another percussion instrument that also carries harmonic information.

Sub-genres of Afrobeats

  • Afropop — most accessible crossover form. Western pop structure with Afrobeats rhythmic DNA
  • Amapiano — South African origin, featuring log drum bass lines, jazzy piano, and a slower, more hypnotic groove (95–105 BPM)
  • Afro-fusion — blends Afrobeats with R&B, hip-hop, or electronic music. More experimental, less bound by convention
  • Afro-drill — fusion of UK drill's aggressive energy with Afrobeats rhythmic elements. Rising particularly in the UK diaspora

Common Mistakes When Writing Afrobeats

  • Treating it as pop with African percussion — adding shakers and talking drums to a Western pop structure does not create Afrobeats. The rhythmic foundation must be built from the groove up, not retrofitted
  • Missing the dembow pattern — the characteristic bounce of Afrobeats comes from the kick drum placement. Without it, the groove loses its identity
  • Using minor keys unnecessarily — Afrobeats' emotional power comes partly from its bright major tonality. Switching to minor for "drama" undermines the genre's emotional language
  • Overcomplicating the melody — Afrobeats hooks are repetitive and simple by design. The repetition creates hypnotic groove, not boredom
  • Ignoring the call-and-response tradition — production that does not include a vocal or percussive response to the lead melody misses a structural element that defines authentic Afrobeats

Putting It Together

Writing authentic Afrobeats means starting with rhythm. Build the dembow groove first. Layer the percussion. Find your key (likely major). Build a simple, repetitive chord loop with bright extensions. Then bring the vocal melody in — short, repetitive, rhythmically locked — and let the call-and-response tradition guide your arrangement decisions.

Afrobeats is a genre where the groove is the argument. The melody and lyrics elaborate on an emotional state the rhythm has already established. Get the rhythm right and the song has already started working before a single word is sung.
